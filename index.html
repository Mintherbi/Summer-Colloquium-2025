Historical Lineage
The study of neural networks traces back to the foundational work on C. elegans, the first organism to have its complete connectome mapped. This 302-neuron nervous system serves as a crucial reference point for understanding how biological neural networks process information and make decisions.

Challenging Human Free Will: Choice Prediction Research

Scientific research challenging human free will has been conducted for many years already. The groundbreaking study by Soon et al. published in Nature Neuroscience in 2008[1] discovered that human choices could be predicted through brain activity up to 10 seconds before a person consciously decided to press a button. This research analyzed activity in the prefrontal and parietal cortex regions, demonstrating that the brain was already preparing decisions long before conscious awareness occurred.

Furthermore, follow-up research published in Scientific Reports in 2019[2] demonstrated that more sophisticated machine learning algorithms could predict human choices with even higher accuracy. These studies raise profound philosophical and scientific questions about whether the conscious decisions we consider 'free' might actually be the result of unconscious brain activity.

Research beginning in 2008 and 2019 has suggested that human decision-making processes may be far more deterministic than we think. The current development of brain simulation and AI technology in the digital age makes these questions increasingly realistic and urgent concerns.

References:
[1] Soon, C. S., Brass, M., Heinze, H. J., & Haynes, J. D. (2008). Unconscious determinants of free decisions in the human brain. Nature Neuroscience, 11(5), 543-545.
[2] Schurger, A., & Sitt, J. D. (2019). An accumulator model for spontaneous neural activity prior to self-initiated movement. Scientific Reports, 9, 13577.
Brain Simulation: From In Vivo to In Silico

What I find particularly noteworthy is that biological research has progressed beyond the traditional in vivo (in living organisms) and in vitro (in laboratory conditions) approaches to the in silico stage—simulation within computers. This represents a fundamental paradigm shift in how we study and understand biological systems.

Attempts to transfer and simulate the human brain in computers have been underway for a long time. Major international research initiatives demonstrate the global commitment to this ambitious goal:

Human Brain Project (HBP)

A European flagship project (2013-2023) that pioneered digital neuroscience, creating computational models and simulations of brain circuits and regions.

Blue Brain Project

Led by EPFL, this Swiss initiative developed algorithms to build biologically faithful digital copies of brain circuits, including detailed neuron morphologies and connectomes.

EBRAINS

An open research infrastructure providing data, tools, and computing facilities for brain-related research, enabling researchers worldwide to build and simulate neural networks.

Open Brain Institute

A foundation supporting the global community in utilizing simulation neuroscience to study the brain and its diseases through virtual neuroscience labs.

These projects represent more than technological achievements—they embody a new scientific methodology where computational simulation becomes a primary research tool alongside traditional experimental approaches. The ability to create digital replicas of neural circuits, test hypotheses in silico, and simulate brain functions at multiple scales opens unprecedented possibilities for understanding consciousness, decision-making, and the very nature of mind.

In Silico Revolution: The transition from studying brains in living organisms (in vivo) and laboratory dishes (in vitro) to computer simulations (in silico) represents a fundamental shift in neuroscience methodology, enabling researchers to explore questions about consciousness and free will that were previously impossible to investigate experimentally.
Neurosecurity: Protecting Free Will in the Digital Age

As our understanding of neural mechanisms deepens and brain-computer interfaces become more sophisticated, a new field has emerged to address the critical need for protecting human cognitive autonomy. Neurosecurity represents the intersection of neuroscience, cybersecurity, and ethics, focusing on safeguarding the most fundamental aspects of human identity and decision-making.

"The protection of the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of neural devices from malicious parties with the goal of preserving the safety of a person's neural mechanisms, neural computation, and free will"
— Ienca, M., & Haselager, P. (2016). Hacking the brain: brain-computer interfacing technology and the ethics of neurosecurity. Neurosurgical Focus, 40(5), E13.[3]

This definition encapsulates the urgent need to develop protective measures as neural technologies advance and become more integrated into daily life.

The historical trajectory from early connectome mapping through advanced brain simulation to predictive decision algorithms culminates in this critical question: How do we preserve human agency in an age where thoughts and decisions can be monitored, predicted, and potentially influenced? Neurosecurity emerges not just as a technical challenge, but as a fundamental requirement for maintaining human dignity and autonomy in the digital age.
